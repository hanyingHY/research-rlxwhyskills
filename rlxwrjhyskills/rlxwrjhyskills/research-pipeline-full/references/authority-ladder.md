# Authority Ladder

## Objective

Prefer trustworthy evidence without confusing discovery sources with retained-evidence sources.

## Default authority order

1. domain-leading peer-reviewed journals, flagship conference proceedings, or official standards when the field uses them as primary authority
2. strong peer-reviewed journals, respected lab or institution reports, and practitioner research with transparent methods and provenance
3. direct same-domain or same-environment studies with clear sample design or evaluation protocol
4. strong working papers, preprints, or benchmark-owner publications from recognized institutions, retained only with explicit caveats
5. discovery indexes, search engines, and aggregators for routing only
6. unreviewed or weakly sourced material only as inspiration, not as stand-alone core evidence

## Domain-specific override rule

In some fields, official specifications, regulatory guidance, or benchmark-owner documentation can outrank journals. State that override explicitly when you use it.

## Use in retained evidence

Retained core evidence should come mainly from levels 1 to 4.

If level 5 or 6 material is retained, the report must explain:

1. why higher-authority replacements were not available
2. why the transfer argument is still worth keeping

## Downgrade triggers

1. anonymous or unclear authorship
2. no stable institution or provenance
3. unclear sample period
4. task or environment mismatch
5. generic enthusiasm without measurable result
